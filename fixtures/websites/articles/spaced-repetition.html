Spaced repetition and long-term retention

By Dana Okafor · June 2, 2026

Spaced repetition is a study technique in which review sessions are separated by increasing intervals of time. Research on human memory consistently finds that spacing practice across days produces stronger long-term retention than massing the same amount of practice into a single session. The effect has been replicated across vocabulary learning, mathematics procedures, and motor skills.

In a typical spaced schedule, a learner reviews new material after one day, then after three days, then after a week, with the interval growing each time the material is recalled successfully. When recall fails, the interval is shortened and the item is practiced again sooner. Software implementations adjust these intervals automatically based on each learner's performance.

The spacing effect was first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885, who measured how quickly memorized syllables were forgotten over time. His forgetting curve showed that memory decays rapidly at first and more slowly later, and that each successful review flattens the curve.

Modern classroom studies report that students who spread five hours of study across two weeks outperform students who spend the same five hours the night before an exam, particularly on tests taken weeks later. The benefit for immediate tests is smaller, which is one reason cramming persists.

Practical guidance

Practical implementations of spaced repetition recommend keeping individual review sessions short, mixing related topics within a session, and testing recall actively instead of re-reading notes. Active retrieval during a review strengthens the memory more than passive exposure does.

Combining spacing with retrieval practice yields larger gains than either technique alone, according to multiple controlled experiments in secondary schools.
