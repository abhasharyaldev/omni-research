The science of effective studying

By Miguel Aranda · June 15, 2026

Decades of cognitive science research point to a small set of study strategies with strong evidence behind them. Retrieval practice, spacing, and interleaving consistently outperform re-reading and highlighting in controlled comparisons. Despite this, surveys of students find that re-reading remains the most common study strategy.

Retrieval practice means closing the book and attempting to recall the material from memory, for example with flashcards or practice questions. The act of retrieving information strengthens the memory trace itself, a phenomenon known as the testing effect.

Interleaving means mixing different problem types within one practice session rather than practicing one type repeatedly. Students who interleave mathematics problem types score higher on delayed tests, even though interleaved practice feels harder while it is happening.

Worked examples help novices most: studying a fully solved problem before attempting similar problems reduces cognitive load and speeds early learning. As expertise grows, the advantage of worked examples fades and independent problem solving becomes more effective.

What does not work well

Highlighting, underlining, and re-reading produce a feeling of familiarity that students often mistake for learning. On delayed tests, these passive techniques show little to no advantage over doing nothing beyond the first reading.

Sleep also plays a measurable role: learners who sleep between study and test recall more than learners who stay awake for the same interval.
